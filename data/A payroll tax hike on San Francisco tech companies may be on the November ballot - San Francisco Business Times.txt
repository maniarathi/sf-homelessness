San Francisco supervisors want to put a 1.5 percent payroll tax on
November's ballot that would hit San Francisco-based tech companies to
help address the city's housing shortage and chronic homelessness.

The tax would generate $120 million in revenue – calculated based on
tech companies' existing payroll expenses – that would be redirected to
homelessness programs and anti-displacement initiatives, according to an
estimate by the city's comptroller.

Supervisor Eric Mar co-authored a proposal to add a 1.5 percent payroll
tax to tech companies in San Francisco to support housing and homeless
programs.

“After five years of a rapid tech boom, we are putting forward what
we’re calling the Homeless and Housing Impact Technology Tax to require
big tech companies to pay their share,” said Supervisor Eric Mar
<http://www.bizjournals.com/sanfrancisco/search/results?q=Eric%20Mar>,
who introduced the measure Tuesday. It was co-authored by Supervisors
Aaron Peskin
<http://www.bizjournals.com/sanfrancisco/search/results?q=Aaron%20Peskin> and
David Campos
<http://www.bizjournals.com/sanfrancisco/search/results?q=David%20Campos>.

In addition to adding a tax of 1.5 percent on the payroll expenses of
tech companies operating in San Francisco, the measure also proposes
lowering business registration fees on small businesses with gross
receipts of $1 million or less. Overall, a total of 46,726 employees
worked for the top 100 San Francisco tech companies as of January 2016.

Advocates for the measure – including Mar, and representatives from the
Coalition on Homeless, Jobs with Justice, and an assortment of other
community groups – said the measure was intended to counterbalance the
effects of a historic tech boom on the city’s housing supply.

"The tech boom has priced out residents and small merchants," said Mar.
"The surge in housing demand brought on by the tech sector and its
highly paid employees make it increasingly difficult for many residents
and families to remain in their homes."

The Bay Area Council, the Housing Action Coalition and other
pro-development groups argue that city officials have created a housing
shortage by making it extremely difficult to develop residential
projects that might house the influx of new workers.

The measure identifies the tech companies using NAICS federal tax codes,
whereby companies self-identify by industry: Those in the computer,
software publishing, data services, Internet, and computer systems
design industries would be subject to the new tax. The new 1.5 percent
tax, levied in addition to the gross receipt tax imposed on companies
currently, was characterized as the most accurate way to hone in on
larger tech companies who employ large numbers of San Franciscans.

“There's lot of conversation that’s going to happen between now and
August," said Nick Pagoulatos, Mar's aide. "We want to make sure that
this will stand out as an solution to a very specific set of problems,
and we want to make sure that the areas we’re funding are those that are
most in need currently.” Those specific areas may include homeless
navigation centers, "small site acquisition" initiatives that stave off
evictions, or other existing housing or homelessness programs that need
funding, he said.

In recent years, tech companies in San Francisco have enjoyed a
friendlier tax climate: In 2012, the city replaced its payroll tax with
a gross receipts tax, which was regarded as favorable to the high-growth
tech industry. The year prior, Mayor Ed Lee had also enacted the
so-called ‘Twitter tax break,’ which eliminated taxes associated with
payroll and stock options for companies in the Mid-Market area,
including Twitter, Zendesk, and others.

The move generated hundreds of millions of dollars of real estate
investment in Mid-Market, boosting property tax collection for the city.

The Homeless and Housing Impact Technology Tax proposal is intended to
balance, not nullify those tax breaks, said Mar: "I think in the 5 years
of the tech boom, there’s been incredible benefits, and I think the city
needs to address (the tax breaks) as that sunsets in 2017. But this is a
way for assessing the companies' fair share."

Mar appeared confident that the measure would gain enough support on the
Board of Supervisors to land on the November ballot. But the measure is
likely to face opposition from groups like SF.Citi, an advocacy group
for San Francisco tech companies.

"Another veiled attempt at division, vilifying an industry that has
played a significant role in ensuring San Francisco has the lowest
unemployment rate in the nation," said Alex Tourk, a spokesman for
SF.Citi. "There are simply more productive ways to find common ground to
benefit the City other using legislative measures to attack as political
payback."

The proposal is subject to revision and a final majority vote by the
Board of Supervisors, which will most likely happen in early August.

In the meantime, “we have meetings with different organizations that
will support a fair share tax, even some of the top five tech
companies," Mar added.
