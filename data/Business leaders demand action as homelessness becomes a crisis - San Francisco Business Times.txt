This is a significant number. Given the fact that San Francisco hosted
24.6 million visitors in 2015, this means 6 million visitors likely
would not return if the current situation on our sidewalks persists. If
San Francisco were to lose 6 million visitors, it would reduce
visitor-induced economic activity in San Francisco by $2.3 billion. It
would translate to 19,130 fewer jobs and reduce visitor-induced
contributions to the city’s general fund by $184 million.

We acknowledge and appreciate the efforts Mayor Lee and city departments
have devoted to tackling these issues. We need to ensure there is
adequate funding for effective programs and efforts aimed at addressing
homelessness and issues associated with street life. Both the business
and hospitality community contribute tax revenue to the city’s general
fund that far exceed budgeted expenditures to homeless services.

Business tax collections for fiscal year 2016-2017 are anticipated to
reach $671 million in the city’s 2016-17 budget, an increase of 50
percent over the past four years. The hotel tax contributes $412 million
to the city’s general fund, and the business and retail community
contributed a combined $1.083 billion to the city’s budget. This is more
than four times what the city spends on homeless related services.

With this in mind, we cannot jeopardize the quality of life of our
residents, our visitors, our employees and our customers. Having clean
streets and safe sidewalks should be a right.

The city must focus on transitioning people off the streets and into
shelter and housing where they can access services that match their
individual needs. We must address the crime and unruly behavior issues
in San Francisco’s neighborhoods. This means fully staffing our police
department to at least its charter-mandated 1,971 officers and
significantly increasing foot patrols. It means actually enforcing
existing laws and creating a system of accountability for everyone. And
it means working proactively with the community to address
neighborhood-specific crime issues.

The business community stands with our city government on addressing the
current problem of uncivil and unsafe streets and we look forward to
working together to create solutions. We need the city to take a
holistic and proactive approach to address homelessness that includes
the issues associated with street behavior. If we don’t, the loss to our
city may be irreparable.
